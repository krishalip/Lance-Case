Problem statement

Guest sentiment and reviews are an underused operational signal

Every review contains a signal about what broke — in housekeeping, F&B, at the front desk. But in most hotels, that signal goes nowhere. It gets responded to, not acted on. The same failure happens to the next guest.

Three problems, one broken loop

At Sonder, I saw this pattern across properties repeatedly. The root cause wasn't effort — GMs were working hard. It was structural. Three problems stacked on top of each other.

01
The picture lives across too many tools
To get any sense of how guests are feeling, a GM has to open Google, TripAdvisor, Booking.com, Expedia — separately, manually, with no aggregation. Each platform shows a slice. None shows the picture. The GM either spends 20+ minutes a day tab-switching, or they don't look at all and rely on what surfaces in conversation.
02
Everything arrives flat — noise and signal look identical
Not every review is actionable. Not every complaint is a pattern. But there's no filter — a one-off noise complaint and a third consecutive F&B failure land the same way. The meaningful signals get lost in volume. And even when the GM spots something important, there's no mechanism to turn that insight into a task, a process change, or a training moment. The loop never closes.
03
By the time it's a review, the damage is already done
A guest who posts a 2-star review had a bad experience 48–72 hours ago. The recovery window passed. But the damage compounds: OTA algorithms surface lower-rated properties less prominently in search, reducing visibility before a prospective guest makes any decision. A score that slips from 4.3 to 4.0 isn't a number — it's a ranking, a conversion rate, and a revenue line.

Every step from trip idea to checkout is well-served by existing tools. The broken link is the one after: turning what a guest experienced into something the next guest doesn't have to go through again.

User & persona

The most accountable person in the building is also the most time-strapped

The GM is ultimately responsible for the guest experience — but they're juggling staff, operations, and commercial pressures simultaneously. Sentiment management gets deprioritised precisely when it matters most.

Who handles reviews depends on property size

At large branded chains, dedicated customer service agents handle review responses day-to-day. At independent or boutique properties, it falls to operations managers or the GM directly. But regardless of who handles routine responses, the reviews that matter most — the ones that drag down a rating or go viral — always escalate to the GM.

The GM needs to be across two things at once: the overall trend (is sentiment improving or slipping?) and the critical individual cases (which guests had genuinely bad experiences and need a personal response?). Neither is easy when the data lives across four platforms and the context lives in people's heads.

Jobs to be done

01
When an escalation lands, understand the full context immediately
Who was on duty, whether there was a maintenance log, what was communicated at check-in — without asking four different people.
02
Know whether a complaint is a one-off or a pattern
A single complaint about slow housekeeping is noise. Six in three weeks is a staffing problem. The GM needs to see both in context.
03
Intercept a bad experience before it becomes a bad review
If a waiter logged a complaint at dinner, can the GM send a personal note and a room credit before the guest checks out — and before they open TripAdvisor?
04
Give the team credit when sentiment is trending up
Positive reviews are a management tool. When guests call out specific staff members, the GM needs to see that and share it — not lose it in a platform feed.

The current workflow delivers on none of these reliably. Lance is built around all four.

Core features — MVP

Four features. Two structural differentiators. One workflow.

MVP scope is deliberately narrow — everything serves the GM's morning review workflow. No dashboards for regional VPs, no brand benchmarking, no employee surveys. Those come later.

Unified conversation inbox
Pre-stay, in-stay, post-stay messages and reviews in one prioritised queue. Customer service handles the routine layer; the GM sees only what needs their attention.
One queue replaces four platforms
Core differentiator
Context brief assembly
Before the GM reads a single message, Lance pulls PMS data, housekeeping notes, maintenance tickets, shift logs, and prior guest history into a plain-English brief.
Full picture before the first word is typed
Core differentiator
AI draft with suggested actions
Not just what to say — what to do. Draft built from the stay record, tone-adjustable, with one-tap action execution for room moves, F&B credits, and ops tasks.
Response and resolution in the same flow
Operational follow-through
Every conversation generates trackable ops tasks routed to the right department. Full lifecycle: created → assigned → acknowledged → resolved. GM sees what's open and overdue without chasing.
Insight closes the loop, not just the inbox

The two features marked as core differentiators share a common dependency: real-time PMS integration at the property level. This is the technical moat. Medallia cannot close this gap by shipping a feature — it requires a distribution and partnership strategy they have no incentive to build for independent properties.

Success metrics

Measure what the GM cares about, not what's easy to count

The temptation is to measure usage — logins, reviews processed, drafts generated. The metrics that matter connect to the GM's actual outcome: a higher review score and a hotel that stops making the same mistakes.

North star — lagging indicator
Review score
Avg. score across platforms, tracked weekly. A 0.2-point increase in 90 days signals the product is working at the outcome level — not just the workflow level.
Leading indicator 1
94%
Response rate within 48h. Baseline: ~40%. This is the most direct proxy for GM adoption and the fastest signal that Lance is being used daily.
Leading indicator 2
68%
Draft acceptance rate — GM publishes AI draft with <20% edits. Measures whether context-aware drafts are genuinely better, not just faster.
Engagement quality
3.2
Avg. ops tasks created per review. Zero tasks = the feedback loop isn't closing. Target: at least one task per negative review in the first 30 days.
Retention signal
D30
Day-30 retention rate. If GMs are using Lance daily in week 1 but dropping off by week 4, the product is solving the acute pain but not becoming a habit. Target: 80%+ D30.

Guardrail metric: draft edit rate should not fall below 15%. If GMs stop editing drafts entirely, either the AI is too confident or the GMs have stopped reading — both are failure modes for response quality.

Risks & mitigations

Five risks: PMS depth, AI trust, GM habit, staff adoption, and platform access.

Each one could break the core feedback loop if left unmanaged. Here's where the exposure sits and how the product is designed to reduce it.

PMS integration depth
Context-aware drafts require granular stay data — attendant on shift, room events, delay notifications. The prototype mocks this data. Most PMS APIs expose reservation data but not operational logs at that level.
Mitigation: Start with Opera and Cloudbeds (~40% of independent market). Accept reduced context fidelity from partial data and be transparent about it in the UI. Improve draft quality iteratively as integration depth increases — early adopters accept "good enough" if the workflow saves time.
AI response quality & GM trust
If a draft response is factually wrong about the stay, or uses a tone the GM wouldn't use, the GM stops using it. One bad draft can break the habit loop permanently for that property.
Mitigation: Always surface the PMS source data the draft was built from — the prototype already does this via the stay context panel. Make editing frictionless. Track edit rate as the primary quality signal: above 80% means the AI is adding friction, not value.
GM daily habit formation
The workflow only works if the GM opens Lance every morning. The prototype addresses this with a mobile-first Morning Brief, but push notifications aren't yet built — meaning the GM still has to remember to open it.
Mitigation: Morning push notification (roadmap: 3–6 months) with review count and score movement is the critical missing piece. Until then, rely on the Brief's 2-minute interaction ceiling and the urgency framing (checkout deadlines, overnight reviews) to build the habit before notifications are live.
Staff task adoption
The ops loop only closes if department heads actually complete the tasks created from reviews. If tasks sit unacknowledged in the board, the GM loses confidence in the system — and the core value proposition (insight-to-action) fails silently.
Mitigation: The supervisor task view (roadmap: 3–6 months) lets dept. heads see and action their queue without needing a Lance login — removing the adoption barrier for staff who aren't the primary user. Until then, track task acknowledgement rate as a leading indicator of loop health.
OTA platform API access
Booking.com and Expedia control their review APIs and can restrict access or change terms. Google's review API has historically been limited for third-party aggregators.
Mitigation: Launch with Google and TripAdvisor (highest GM priority, more accessible APIs). Use scraping as a fallback where APIs are restricted. Build platform diversity from the start — no single source should be load-bearing.

The setup risk worth flagging: ops task routing depends on knowing the hotel's org structure — who owns what department, who the right supervisor is. That's a configuration cost at onboarding. But it's also a switching cost once done, which strengthens retention. The first 30 minutes a GM spends setting up Lance is the best retention investment in the product.

Design rationale

TL;DR

The decisions that shaped this product, stated directly.

01
Chosen user
I chose the GM — specifically at an independent property under 100 rooms — because they're the only person in the building with both the context to understand why something went wrong and the authority to make sure it doesn't happen again. Customer service can respond. Department heads can act. But only the GM closes the loop between guest experience and operations. That makes them uniquely valuable as a user, and uniquely frustrated by tools that only do half the job.

The sub-100 room constraint matters. At smaller properties, there's no dedicated guest experience team to triage reviews, no customer service layer to absorb the volume, and no enterprise platform stitching the signals together. The GM is doing all of it — manually, across four platforms, usually before 9am. Larger chains have the same problem in theory, but they also have workarounds: headcount, tooling, process. This user has the problem and nothing else.
02
Chosen workflow
I focused on the moment between review arrival and operational response. The GM reads a review and has to decide: what actually happened, who do I message, what do I change? That 10-minute window — repeated daily — is where value leaks. Everything else (aggregation, dashboards, reporting) is infrastructure. This moment is the product.
03
Why mobile
GMs aren't at desks — they're on the floor, in the kitchen, walking rooms. If the workflow requires sitting down, it won't happen consistently. I designed for mobile because it makes review triage ambient: open the Brief at 7am, respond before the morning meeting, close the loop before the day starts. A desktop tool becomes another platform to check. A mobile habit becomes the default.
04
MVP scope
I scoped the MVP to three things: (1) a unified Morning Brief pulling reviews from 4 OTA platforms, (2) AI-drafted responses with PMS context attached, (3) one-tap task creation routing to the right department. Weekly Intelligence, Channels breakdown, and staff praise messaging are Layer 2. The MVP only needs to prove the daily habit loop: review arrives, response goes out, task gets created.
05
How messaging fits
I included messaging not as a communications feature but as the mechanism that closes the loop without the GM leaving the product. When a review reveals a failure, they flag it directly to the responsible person — and that message creates a task in Ops. The insight stays in the system. Without this, the review gets responded to publicly but the internal follow-up happens over Slack or verbally, and may not happen at all.
06
What AI should and should not do
I want AI to draft the response (specific to this guest, this stay, this context) and surface patterns across reviews. I don't want it to publish without GM confirmation, assign blame to specific staff, or create tasks autonomously. The GM is accountable for what goes public. AI earns trust incrementally — it starts as a draft tool, not a decision-maker.
07
Actions requiring confirmation
I set two hard confirms: publishing a response publicly (one wrong word on a bad review makes it worse), and sending a staff task from a review (the GM should see what's being created before it hits someone's queue). Soft confirms: advancing a task to Done — immediate, with undo. Silent: marking a review as read, logging a draft — no friction needed.
08
Intentional omissions
I left out guest-facing messaging, multi-property management, owner reporting, revenue attribution modelling, and deep CRM integration. All real future directions — each adds scope without proving the core loop. I also excluded AI that modifies a published response or suggests score targets. I'm not building a tool to game platforms. I'm building one to fix the operation behind them.
09
What I'd test with hotel staff
Three things: (1) Does the AI draft reduce editing time or increase it — what's the edit rate and average edit length? (2) Do tasks created from reviews actually get actioned, or do they sit unacknowledged? (3) Does the GM open the app 4+ days in the first two weeks — and what causes them to skip it? The third one is the habit risk. Three missed days in week one and the product is effectively dead for that property.
10
Kill, narrow, or expand
Kill: if GMs aren't opening it 4+ days/week after onboarding, or if the AI draft edit rate is above 80% — the tool is adding friction, not value. Narrow: if the only high-retention cohort is independent boutique properties under 100 rooms, I'd stop trying to serve chains and own that segment completely. Expand: if the ops task loop is consistently completing — tasks acknowledged and resolved within 48h — that's the signal to build deeper department-level features: shift logs, compliance tracking, staff performance tied to review themes. The second expansion I'd pursue is a franchise manager layer: the same product, one level up. A franchise manager overseeing 8–15 branded properties doesn't need to read individual reviews — they need to see which GMs are responding, which properties are slipping, and where the same operational failure is recurring across locations. The Morning Brief becomes a portfolio brief. The sentiment engine becomes a quality signal across the whole estate.
